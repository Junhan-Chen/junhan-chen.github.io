# Project Overview

This coursework project focused on the interpretation and production of engineering drawings through both manual sketching and CAD-based modelling. The work combined orthographic projection, isometric sketching, three-dimensional modelling in Creo, and an original design task.

The project was divided into three parts: hand-drawn multiview and isometric drawings of given components, CAD reconstruction of mechanical parts from dimensioned drawings, and the design of a guitar holder with corresponding multiview documentation. These tasks developed core skills in engineering drawing, spatial visualisation, and design communication.

---

# Assignment Structure

According to the assignment brief, the project consisted of three sections:

- **Section A:** Orthographic drawing and isometric sketching of three engineering solids by hand  
- **Section B:** 3D modelling of two given objects using Creo, followed by multiview drawings for manufacture  
- **Section C:** Original design of a guitar holder in Creo, with engineering drawings and design explanation  

Section A accounted for 30% of the coursework, Section B for 50%, and Section C for 20%. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

# Section A – Orthographic Drawing and Isometric Sketching

Section A focused on the relationship between multiview drawings and three-dimensional understanding of engineering solids. Three components were provided in orthographic view, and the task required the production of complete engineering drawings, including missing views and isometric sketches. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Multiview drawings typically include the front view, top view, and right-side view. By analysing the given views and identifying the geometry of each component, the missing projection could be inferred and an isometric sketch could then be constructed. This process required careful interpretation of spatial relationships and hidden features, as described in the report. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

For Component A, the front and right-side views were first interpreted and used to construct the isometric representation. For Components B and C, auxiliary construction lines were used to derive the missing top views before completing the isometric sketches. This improved the readability of the object geometry and supported more accurate visualisation. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

## Benefits of Isometric Sketching

The report discusses isometric sketching as an effective way to represent three-dimensional geometry while preserving proportional relationships along the principal axes. Because all three axes are treated consistently, isometric drawings provide a clearer understanding of spatial structure than a single orthographic view alone. They also improve communication efficiency by allowing multiple dimensions of an object to be understood in one image. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

## Comparison with Oblique Sketching

The coursework also compared isometric sketches with oblique sketches. In the report, isometric projection is presented as more suitable for technical communication because it preserves proportional consistency, whereas oblique drawing is easier and faster to sketch but may distort depth and reduce dimensional realism. This makes oblique sketches more suitable for conceptual illustration, while isometric sketches are more suitable for engineering representation. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

# Section B – 3D Modelling of Given Objects

Section B focused on reconstructing two mechanical parts as three-dimensional CAD models in Creo based on the given dimensioned engineering drawings. The assignment required the final drawings to contain sufficient information for fabrication, including all views necessary for manufacture. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Creo Parametric was used as the main CAD tool. According to the report, the modelling process involved interpreting the geometry from the provided drawings, building parametric sketches, and then using standard modelling commands such as **Extrude**, **Hole**, **Plane**, and **Mirror**. Additional sketch tools such as line, circle, deleted segment, and equality constraints were also used to define the profiles and maintain geometric consistency. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

After the 3D models were completed, multiview drawings were generated from the models to produce technical documentation suitable for manufacturing. This part of the project highlighted the connection between conventional engineering drawing and modern CAD workflows.

## Benefits of Multiview Drawings

The report identifies multiview drawings as essential for accurate engineering communication because they provide detailed and precise descriptions of dimensions and geometry. By combining front, top, and side views, they reduce ambiguity and support manufacturing more effectively than pictorial or perspective drawings. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

## CAD Applications

A further discussion in the report highlights the importance of CAD software in modern engineering design. Compared with manual drawing, CAD enables more precise geometry creation, easier modification, and more efficient handling of complex features. In this project, Creo served both as a modelling platform and as a means of generating technical drawings from the final 3D models. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

# Section C – Guitar Holder Design

The final section required the original design of a guitar holder using Creo, together with an engineering drawing for manufacture and an explanation of the design choices. The assignment brief specifically asked for both the CAD model and its orthographic projection. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

The design process began with considering the basic requirements of the holder, including stability, safety, and adaptability. The report explains that dimensions and structural form were selected so that the holder could support a guitar securely without damaging the instrument surface. After the concept was established, the design was modelled in Creo and orthographic projections were generated from the final CAD model. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

## Design Explanation

The report gives three key design considerations:

### 1. Triangular structural stability

A triangular structural arrangement was adopted to improve stability and reduce the likelihood of tipping under minor disturbance. This was a central design decision for ensuring safe guitar placement. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

### 2. Rounded contact edges

All possible edges that may come into contact with the guitar were rounded with a radius of 5 mm. This was done to reduce the risk of scratches or local damage when the instrument is placed on the holder. :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}

### 3. Adjustable mechanical feature

The final design also incorporated a simple mechanical adjustment mechanism. By placing the locator in different slots, the angle between the legs of the stand could be changed, making the holder more adaptable to different guitars and usage situations. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}

# Key Learning Outcomes

This project developed several core engineering skills:

- interpretation of orthographic and multiview drawings  
- production of isometric sketches from 2D views  
- 3D CAD modelling using Creo  
- generation of manufacturing-oriented multiview drawings  
- design thinking for functionality, safety, and adaptability  

More broadly, the coursework strengthened the connection between technical drawing, spatial imagination, and CAD-based engineering design, which are foundational skills for later work in mechanical and product engineering. This aligns closely with the assignment learning outcomes, which emphasised engineering drawing standards, orthographic projection, isometric representation, spatial visualisation, CAD applications, and engineering-style reporting. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}

---

# Conclusion

This coursework project combined hand-drawn engineering graphics, CAD reconstruction of given parts, and an original design task into a single workflow. Through Section A, Section B, and Section C, the project demonstrated how engineering drawings can be interpreted, constructed, and translated into three-dimensional models and manufacturing documentation.

Overall, the project provided a practical introduction to engineering drawing and CAD modelling, while also reinforcing the importance of precision, readability, and design intent in technical communication.
